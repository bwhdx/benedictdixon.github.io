Brown butter chocolate chip cookies

The diner cookie, refined. Browned butter and a 24-hour rest do the heavy lifting.

Yield
makes 18 cookies
Total time
26h
Active time
45m
Difficulty
moderate

Ingredients

dough

  • 227g1 cup unsalted butter
  • 200g1 cup light brown sugar, packed
  • 100g1/2 cup granulated sugar
  • 2 large2 large eggs, room temperature
  • 1 large1 large egg yolk, room temperature
  • 10ml2 tsp vanilla extract
  • 315g2 1/2 cups all-purpose flour
  • 5g1 tsp baking soda
  • 6g1 1/4 tsp fine sea salt
  • 300g2 cups dark chocolate, chopped, 60–70% cacao

to finish

  • flakyflaky Maldon salt, to taste

Method

  1. 1

    Brown the butter in a light-coloured pan over medium heat. Swirl constantly once it starts to foam. It's done when the milk solids at the bottom are deep amber and the whole pan smells like toffee — about 6–8 minutes. Pour into a heatproof bowl, scraping in every brown bit, and cool to barely warm.

  2. 2

    Whisk the sugars into the warm butter until smooth and glossy. Add the eggs and yolk one at a time, whisking well between each. Stir in the vanilla.

  3. 3

    In a separate bowl, whisk the flour, baking soda, and salt. Fold into the wet mixture with a spatula until no streaks remain. Fold in the chopped chocolate.

  4. 4

    Cover and refrigerate for at least 24 hours. This is non-negotiable — the rest is what makes the flavour.

  5. 5

    When ready to bake, heat the oven to 190°C / 375°F. Line two baking sheets with parchment.

  6. 6

    Scoop 60g portions (about 2 heaped tablespoons) onto the sheets, spacing well apart. Sprinkle each with flaky salt.

  7. 7

    Bake one tray at a time for 11–13 minutes — edges set and golden, centres still soft and a little underdone. Cool on the tray for 5 minutes before moving.

Notes

  • The 24-hour rest is doing real work — the flour hydrates, the flavours deepen. Don't skip.
  • Pull them a minute early for chewy centres; a minute later for crisp edges.
  • Dough keeps in the fridge for 72 hours and freezes (pre-scooped) for a month.

I make these cookies more than any other recipe in this kitchen. They started as a weekend project — I wanted to understand why the cookies from a particular diner I love taste the way they do — and they ended up as the default cookie I make when someone's coming over and I haven't planned anything.

Why brown butter

Browning the butter pulls the milk solids past their usual cream-coloured state and into a deep, toasted amber. The flavour shift is enormous — there's a hazelnut, almost-toffee note that doesn't exist in a regular cookie. The trade-off is that you lose some of the moisture from the butter (water boils off during browning), so the cookie ends up denser and chewier. That's the goal here.

Why the 24-hour rest

This isn't precious advice — the rest is doing something specific. The flour fully hydrates, the proteins relax, and a small amount of enzymatic activity breaks complex starches into simpler sugars. The result tastes deeper and bakes more evenly. If you have to skip it, you can; the cookies will still be good. But once you've tried them rested, you won't go back.

On chocolate

Chopped bar chocolate gives you irregular shards and pools of melt — far better than chips, which are formulated to hold their shape and don't melt the same way. I use a 70% bar for most batches; a 60% if I want them sweeter. Use what you like, but chop it.

The salt question

Fine salt in the dough does the bakery job — balancing sweet. Flaky salt on top is the finishing touch and the thing people remember. Don't skip it.
